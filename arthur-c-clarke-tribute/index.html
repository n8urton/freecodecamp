Timeline of Sir Clarke's Life

  • 1917 - Born in Minehead, England
  • 1936 - Moved to London, joining the Board of Education
  • 1942-1946 - Served in the Royal Air Force, contributing to early-warning radar defence system working on ground-controlled approach radar
  • 1945 - Wireless World published Clarke's paper on the use of geostationary satellites as ideal telecommunication relays
  • 1946 - Rescue Party published in Astounding Science Fiction, Clarke's first professional literary sale
  • 1946-1947 - Served as Chairman of the British Interplanetar Society
  • 1948 - Wrote "The Sentinel" for a BBC competition. It was rejected, but would form the basis for 2001: A Space Odyssey
  • 1953 - Married Marilyn Mayfield
  • 1956 - Discovered underwater artifacts of the Koneswaram Temple
  • 1961 - Develeped paralysis, later found to be caused by Polio
  • 1964 - Divorced of Marylyn Mayfield finalized
  • 1956- Moved to Sri Lanka, possibly due to more tolarant environment towards homosexuality
  • 1969 - Appears as CBS commentator for the Apollo 11 moon landing
  • 1970 - Became the first science fiction writer to sign a three book publishing deal
  • 1984 - Clarke testified before Congess against the Strategic Defense Initiative
  • 1986 - In an interview with Playboy Magazine, answered "Of course. Who hasn't." when asked if he had any bisexual experience
  • 1998 - Accused of pedophilia by British tabloid The Sunday Mirror. Charge was found to be baseless by Sri Lankan authorities and the Mirror would later publish an apology
  • 2000 - Annointed Knight Bachelor for services to literature
  • 2004 - After his Arthur C. Clark Diving School at Hikkaduwa was destroyed in the 2004 Indian Ocean Earthquake Tsunami, he made humaitarian appeals along with his Arthur C. Clark Foundation, worked toward better disaster notification systems.
  • 2008 - On March 19th, Sir Clarke passed away due to respiratory complications and heart failure stemming from post-polio syndrome. Coincidentally, a massive gamma-ray bust reached Earth Earth just hours before Clarke's death (GRB 080319b) and set a new record as the farthest object that could be seen from Earth with the naked eye. It was suggested this even be named "The Clarke Event", but was deemed unnecessary as stated by Astronomer Phil Plait, "The poetic alignment of the two events is enough for me, to be honest."
